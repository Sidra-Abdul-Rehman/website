Why It Matters

The Power of Early Physiotherapy

The first three years of life represent the most critical window for neurological development. During this period, the brain is at its most plastic — capable of remarkable adaptation and learning.

  • Improves muscle tone, strength, and coordination
  • Supports achievement of motor developmental milestones
  • Reduces secondary complications of neurological conditions
  • Empowers parents with home exercise programs
  • Enhances quality of life for the entire family

Child Conditions

Cerebral Palsy

Neurological
↓

What is it?

Cerebral Palsy (CP) is a group of permanent neurological disorders affecting movement, muscle tone, and posture, caused by damage to the developing brain — most often before or during birth.

Common Signs

  • Delayed motor milestones (sitting, walking)
  • Abnormal muscle tone (too stiff or too floppy)
  • Asymmetric use of arms and legs
  • Difficulty with balance and coordination
  • Abnormal gait patterns

Role of Physiotherapy

Physiotherapy is central to CP management. Therapists use neurodevelopmental techniques, stretching, strengthening, gait training, and adaptive equipment to maximize mobility, prevent contractures, and improve functional independence.

🧩

Down Syndrome

Chromosomal
↓

What is it?

Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21) is a chromosomal condition caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21. It affects physical growth, cognitive development, and is associated with characteristic physical features.

Common Signs

  • Generalized hypotonia (low muscle tone)
  • Delayed motor development
  • Joint hypermobility
  • Short stature
  • Cognitive and learning differences

Role of Physiotherapy

Physiotherapy addresses the hypotonia that is central to Down Syndrome, supporting the development of head control, sitting, standing, and walking. Strengthening, balance training, and orthotic prescription are key components of the management plan.

💧

Hydrocephalus

Neurological
↓

What is it?

Hydrocephalus is a condition in which excess cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) accumulates in the brain's ventricles. This buildup increases pressure in the skull and can damage brain tissue, affecting motor and cognitive development.

Common Signs

  • Abnormally large head circumference
  • Delayed achievement of motor milestones
  • Spasticity or abnormal tone
  • Visual and balance difficulties
  • Irritability and poor feeding in infants

Role of Physiotherapy

Post-surgical and ongoing physiotherapy helps children with hydrocephalus develop optimal motor function, manage secondary complications like spasticity, and achieve developmental milestones through structured rehabilitation programs.

🔬

Microcephaly

Neurological
↓

What is it?

Microcephaly is a condition where a baby's head is significantly smaller than expected due to abnormal brain development. It can occur as an isolated condition or as part of a broader syndrome.

Common Signs

  • Head circumference significantly below average
  • Intellectual disability (variable severity)
  • Delayed motor and speech development
  • Seizures in some cases
  • Feeding difficulties

Role of Physiotherapy

Physiotherapy supports children with microcephaly by facilitating motor development, improving muscle strength and coordination, and maximizing functional independence through individualized, goal-directed therapy programs.

📈

Developmental Delay

Developmental
↓

What is it?

Developmental delay refers to when a child does not reach developmental milestones at the expected age. It can affect motor, speech, cognitive, and social development, and may be temporary or reflect an underlying condition.

Common Signs

  • Not sitting by 9 months, walking by 18 months
  • Persistent primitive reflexes beyond expected age
  • Poor head and trunk control
  • Difficulty with age-appropriate play activities
  • Asymmetric or unusual movement patterns

Role of Physiotherapy

Early physiotherapy assessment identifies areas of delay and designs targeted programs to close the developmental gap. Play-based intervention, sensorimotor activities, and parent training are all key tools in supporting children with developmental delay.
